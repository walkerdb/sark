Voice of America

Founded by the US government in 1942 to counter Nazi radio propaganda abroad, over the course of the war the Voice of America program quickly grew to provide shortwave broadcasts in 40 languages. Though created to transmit news, the stations also played over 1,000 specialized programs, including music and commentary.

Voice of America transferred first to the State Department in 1945, and then to the U.S. Information Agency in 1953. This move ushered in the radio service’s Cold War era, to which the bulk of the Sarkisian recordings belong. With a renewed commitment to counteracting propaganda abroad, the VOA continued to broadcast news from the United States and around the world, targeting nations behind the Iron Curtain. Often the VOA’s most popular and influential programs, however, were not news but music shows, particularly those featuring American jazz musicians. Historians credit programs such as “Music USA”--a two-hour daily broadcast of jazz and pop music--with popularizing jazz outside the U.S., particularly in Soviet nations.

As with its European counterparts, for much of its early existence the Voice of America tactically used American culture to advance U.S. strategic interests on the continent. Leo Sarkisian's "Music Time in Africa" program, however, broke that mold. Instead of pushing American music onto his African listeners, Leo instead played traditional and contemporary music of Africa itself, often in the form of live field recordings captured on one of his many trips through the continent, making a sincere effort to spread knowledge of traditions and the broad musical and cultural diversity to be found in each African nation.

Now administered by the Broadcasting Board of Governors, the Voice of America continues to broadcast to countries around the world. VOA has reduced its number of stations in the past decade, eliminating many of its services to Asia and Europe due to budget cuts and shifting diplomatic priorities. Among the 41 foreign languages still on the air are 13 spoken in African countries, as well as 4 of its English language stations.


Leo Sarkisian & Music Time in Africa

Leo Sarkisian is a remarkable man: an Armenian-American artist and music lover from Lawrence, Massachusetts who became a globetrotting musicologist, world-class recordist, and musical ambassador to millions.

Leo began his professional life in the visual arts. He studied at the Vesper George School of Art, drew battlefield maps for the U.S. Army during the Second World War, and afterwards worked as a freelance illustrator in New York City. But he spent his free time in studying the folk and ethnic music he loved, and wrote a paper on the subject that brought him to the attention of Irving Fogel, head of Tempo Records. Fogel hired Sarkisian, provided him with extensive technical training, and sent him to record world music in Central Asia and then Africa in the 1950s. His excellent work with Tempo convinced Edward Murrow, head of the United States Information Agency, that Sarkisian was the man for the Voice of America's new Africa Division.

It was the early 1960s, and Edward Murrow saw opportunities for Voice of America to expand into Africa. He recognized that if VOA was to broadcast in Africa, it would be logical and necessary to play African music. In 1963, Murrow traveled to Guinea to personally recruit Leo Sarkisian. After a short return to Washington D.C. to complete Foreign Service Officer training and gather equipment, Sarkisian headed to Liberia to begin recording music.

Beginning in 1963, Leo Sarkisian traveled and recorded in 53 African countries, covering nearly all of Africa outside of the Sahara and capturing an enormous variety of music and musicians—from international stars of the big cities to village musicians who had never before been recorded. These recordings have formed the base of Voice of America's radio show Music Time in Africa since 1965.

Once Sarkisian had built up the respectable beginnings of a music library, "Music Time in Africa" began broadcasting in 1965. Sarkisian wrote scripts for the show from the beginning, shipping them back to the US along with "insert" tapes - containing each individual track of music meant to be played for that program. In the 1970s he took on cohosting duties alongside Rita Rochelle Hunt. Throughout that decade and the next the show’s popularity boomed, with the two hosts becoming household names across the African continent. Sarkisian continued to travel through Africa, recording music and meeting fans.

Behind the Tapes

Throughout his recording career, Sarkisian followed the same basic method. Before traveling to any country he researched in depth about its culture, musical styles and prominent personages. Upon arrival, he and his wife Mary generally set up housekeeping and a home office in the largest city. Traveling as employees of the United States government, Leo and Mary moved in diplomatic and government circles, and the connections made there gave them the freedom and support to head out of the city and circle the rural regions, capturing music that in many cases had never been been recorded before. Their own warm, outgoing spirits meant they gathered friends wherever they went.

Leo traveled the rural areas by jeep or van when possible, by foot or donkey when not. He was usually accompanied by a local guide or dignitary to smooth his way and make introductions, and Mary was very often along as well to record the trip, set up microphones, and help explain the proceedings to musicians. Leo made his recordings in every setting from concert halls to forest clearings, but always got the best sound possible from his environment. He might have drummers sit next to the wall, or set up a dozen microphones for a large choral group, and, once in Ghana, a canopy of mango trees acted as a perfect acoustical ceiling. Due to his extensive work in rural areas, he was one of the first to try new portable recording devices as they were developed, and he always made it a point to play the recording back to the musicians afterward!

Now the entirety of the remaining original tapes from Mr. Sarkisian's travels and broadcasts are held by the University of Michigan. Once funding has been secured, we intend to embark on a large-scale digitization and access project, making this material available to anyone with an internet connection.
